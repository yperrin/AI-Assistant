A short Article on how we chose the models we work with

https://www.oreilly.com/radar/the-model-you-love-is-probably-just-the-one-you-use/
Even Shorter summary:
This article by Tim O'Brien argues that our preferences for specific AI models are rarely objective. Instead, they are shaped by access, familiarity, and corporate influence.
Key Takeaways
The Familiarity Bias: Developers often "love" a model simply because they’ve used it the most. Proficiency in prompting a specific LLM creates a sense of "intuitiveness" that is actually just a result of repetitive use rather than technical superiority.
The "Walled Garden" of Enterprise: In corporate settings, model choice is often accidental—driven by whoever had a company credit card or which tool was first approved by procurement—rather than a rigorous "bakeoff" between competitors.
Invisible Influence: Marketing budgets, early access programs, and "curated" influencer demos distort public perception. Many users are reacting to a feeling of prestige or a managed benchmark rather than raw performance.
The "Overthinking" Problem: Using top-tier models (like Opus) for simple tasks can be counterproductive. High-powered models often add unnecessary abstractions to basic code, whereas smaller models (like Haiku) are faster, cheaper, and often more direct.
Geopolitical Friction: There is a growing divide between developers using high-performance, low-cost models from China (like Qwen or GLM) and those avoiding them due to origin concerns.
The Recommendation
O'Brien suggests a one-week minimum threshold for evaluating any new model. Anything less is just a "first impression" filtered through your existing biases. He also highlights that models like Codex are becoming nearly indistinguishable from top-tier models in coding tasks while being significantly more token-efficient.
